# Distribution playbook — Weeks 1–4

The cold-email play didn't work. The reason isn't that the product is wrong; it's that primary-school teachers don't read or respond to cold email from unknown senders, full stop. They evaluate teaching tools through three channels: a colleague tries it, a kid asks for it, or it shows up on a marketplace they already trust (TES, Teachers Pay Teachers, Twinkl).

This four-week plan attacks all three. None of it requires money to start; the only paid component is a £100 ad test in W4 and that only fires if the earlier weeks haven't already produced a teacher who'll case-study us.

---

## W1 — One classroom, in person

The single biggest leverage point right now is getting one teacher to run a real five-minute session with their class. Everything after — content, case study, parent loop, paid — depends on having that one credible reference.

### The shortlist

Pick three schools you can physically walk into this week. Filters:

The school has to be Y1–Y3 (ages 5–7) or a Reception/EYFS class, because that's where the activities are pitched. State primary works better than independent — independent schools route everything through their head of digital, which adds weeks. If you have any personal connection (a parent at the school, a previous client, a friend-of-friend teacher), that school goes top of the list. Otherwise, pick the three closest primaries with a public-facing main reception.

Write the three down with a name and a planned time. Aim for one Wednesday or Thursday before 9am — that's when the staffroom is busiest and the head is most likely to be at the door. Avoid Mondays (everyone's hammered) and Fridays (everyone's checked out).

### The 60-second pitch (memorise this)

"Hi, my name's Justin. I'm not selling anything. I built a free five-minute movement break for primary classes — kids wave their hand at the screen and it teaches letter tracing, sorting, that kind of thing. I'm offering one teacher in this school the chance to try it in a real class this week, in exchange for ten minutes of feedback after. Could I leave a one-pager for whoever covers EYFS or Year 1?"

Three deliberate moves in there: "I'm not selling anything" disarms the gatekeeper; "free" + "five minutes" is the smallest possible ask; "exchange for feedback" gives them a reason to engage that isn't a favour. Don't deviate. If they ask follow-up questions, the one-pager has answers.

### The one-pager you leave

Print the `/teachers/setup` page from Chrome (Cmd+P — it's already formatted for A4 staffroom posters via the print stylesheet). Write across the top in black marker: "Justin · 07XXX XXX XXX · pop me a text after first session". Leave one copy at reception with a sticky note: "For whoever runs Year 1 / Reception — would love their take." The teacher gets the URL on the printout; they don't need a separate landing page.

### The follow-up that day

Whether they say yes, no, or maybe — send an email the same day to `head@<school>.gov.uk` (or whatever's on their website). One paragraph:

> Subject: Movement break I dropped off this morning
>
> Hi — I came in this morning and left a one-pager for the Year 1 / Reception team. It's a free in-browser movement game (drawintheair.com/teachers/setup). Three minutes to set up, takes nothing off your network, no kid accounts. Happy to walk a teacher through it on Zoom if it'd help, or you can just send me feedback to mrjustinukaegbu@gmail.com after they try it. Thanks for the time.

Short. No pitch. No "circling back". Just a respectful nudge with a real URL they can act on.

### Success criterion for W1

One class runs the product, end-to-end, with a real teacher orchestrating. That's the bar. Not "school agrees to a pilot programme", not "head books a meeting" — one teacher, one Tuesday morning, one real session you can ask about afterwards.

If you hit that by Friday, W2 is content. If you don't, repeat W1 — the playbook scales (you can walk into 3 more schools next week) but the next stages depend on having at least one real session.

## W2 — Show your work where teachers already are

You need owned channels because cold inbound doesn't work, and you need shoulder-of-giants channels because nobody knows you yet. Both ship the same week.

### Twitter/X — a 6-tweet thread

The thread is the unit. Write it once, pin it, reuse it. The audience is `#EYFS`, `#PrimaryEducation`, `#TeacherTwitter`, the @EYFSTeacher community, and US equivalents (`#EarlyChildEd`, `#KindergartenTeacher`). Post Tuesday at 19:00 GMT for UK reach, repost the same content Wednesday at 09:00 PST for US reach.

Draft:

> 1/ I built a free movement break for primary classes. Kids wave at the screen, the screen teaches letters / sorting / numbers. Five minutes. Zero accounts. Runs in any browser. Here's why I think it's actually useful 👇
>
> 2/ Brain breaks are how good teachers reset focus after maths. The problem is most brain breaks are either too rowdy (Just Dance) or too watch-only (GoNoodle). Nothing taught while the kids were standing up. This does.
>
> 3/ Sample activities: trace letters in the air, pop math-answer balloons, sort foods vs toys by reaching. Each is 60 seconds. Targets motor skills, recognition, recall — same things they'd do at their desk, but standing.
>
> 4/ One join code per class, four digits. You show it on the board, kids type it on their tablets, you run the room from your laptop. No student accounts, ever. Camera frames never leave the device.
>
> 5/ Teacher quick-start: drawintheair.com/teachers/setup (prints to A4 for the staffroom). Parent version: drawintheair.com/parents/setup. Try in 5 mins, end of the day if you want.
>
> 6/ Free for home + classroom use forever. Schools that want it across multiple classrooms pay (that subsidises the home tier). DMs open if you've got a class that'd test it next week.

Each tweet stands alone in case Twitter throttles the thread. Include the URL only in tweets 5 and 6 — keeping the early tweets URL-free improves reach.

### Success criterion for W2

Twitter thread + 2 LinkedIn posts + TES listing live by end of Friday. No engagement target — the first run of any content baseline is "post it and move on, optimise next cycle". You're seeding inventory, not chasing virality.

## W3 — Make the first session a case study

By end of W2 you should have at least one teacher who ran a real session (from W1 in-person), plus inbound from the TES listing and the LinkedIn posts. W3 turns one of those into a credible artefact.

### Pick the case-study teacher

Best candidate, in order of preference: the W1 walk-in teacher (already in person, real classroom, real kids); a Twitter/LinkedIn responder who has photos/quotes ready; a TES listing inbound (lowest priority — they're least invested in the relationship).

Whoever they are, the ask is: a 20-minute call, three quotes you can use, and permission to post a sanitised classroom photo (no faces visible) plus the school's name only if they explicitly OK it. Otherwise "Reception class in West London, 24 kids".

### The case study itself — 1 page

The structure that converts best for ed-tech is the **before/during/after** framing. Three sections, ~150 words each:

**Before:** the specific lesson moment, the problem the teacher had with brain breaks before, what they were using instead. The reader (another teacher) needs to recognise their own classroom in this paragraph.

**During:** what actually happened in the 5-minute session. Number of kids engaged. What surprised the teacher. One specific student moment (anonymised). Bullet-point the activities used.

**After:** the teacher's verdict in their own words. Quotable line at the top. What they'd change. Whether they're using it again. Permission to share the link with colleagues.

End with two CTAs: "Try the activities your class would do" → `drawintheair.com` and "See the teacher's quick-start" → `drawintheair.com/teachers/setup`.

### Distribution of the case study

The case study lives at `/case-studies/<school-or-class-name>` as a route on the site. Cross-link from:

The TES listing description gets a "See how Mrs. ___'s class used it" link at the bottom. The Twitter pinned thread gets a quote-tweet pinning the case study. LinkedIn gets a second-pass post titled "Update: first classroom ran it last week, here's what happened" linking the case study. The teachers' setup guide gets a small "See a real classroom session" link in the hero. Direct outreach to the W1 list's heads (any school you walked into) gets a "Mrs. ___ at <school> tried it — here's how it went" email with the case study as a single link.

### The 30-second clip

Modern teacher-to-teacher discovery on TikTok and Instagram Reels is driven by clips, not text. The teacher's session is the clip source. With permission (no faces of children, ever), record 30 seconds from a single laptop angle showing:

A 5-second teacher establishing shot ("Hi I'm Mrs ___, Reception class, West London"), then 15 seconds of the teacher's laptop console — picking an activity, kids' silhouettes from behind reaching for the screen, the teacher commenting on engagement, then 10 seconds of the teacher's verdict piece-to-camera.

Post to TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts same day. Caption: "Free 5-min brain break I tested in class this week — drawintheair.com/teachers/setup".

### Success criterion for W3

Case study published at `/case-studies/<name>` by Friday with at least one direct quote, one activity-screenshot, and one teacher-verdict line. Cross-links live on TES, Twitter, LinkedIn, and the teacher setup page.

## W4 — Parent loop + £100 paid test

W4 splits into two parallel tracks that test two different distribution hypotheses: organic parent-to-teacher referral (free, scales virally) and paid acquisition (costs money, predictable).

### The parent loop — measurement, not new build

The parents' setup guide at `/parents/setup` already has a "Show your kid's teacher" button that opens a pre-filled mailto. Most of W4's parent loop work is **instrumenting it** to know if it actually fires, since right now we have no data on the loop's pull.

Wire two events: `parent_share_button_clicked` (when the button is tapped) and `teacher_invite_email_drafted` (immediately after window.location.href fires, indicating the mailto opened). The first is direct; the second is best-effort because we can't observe whether the user actually sent the email — but a mailto-open is a strong intent signal.

Past those, the dashboard cohort retention RPC will tell us if any of the inbound `/teachers/setup` traffic that week came after a `parent_share_button_clicked` event from the same household — same IP within 24h. That's the loop closure metric.

Add a small change to the parents' setup page: a one-line CTA below the share-with-teacher button reading "Once they try it, send them this 1-page guide" linking to the new W3 case study. Drives a second tap, even if the first one doesn't convert.

### The £100 ad test — Meta + Google split

The hypothesis we're testing is **cheap channel**: can we acquire a teacher who runs a real class for under £20? If yes, we have a scalable lever. If no, we know paid isn't the answer and we re-double on organic.

**Budget split:** £60 to Meta (Facebook + Instagram), £40 to Google Search. Reasoning: Meta is better at finding parents-of-primary-kids who'll forward to teachers; Google captures the active-intent teacher searching for "movement break classroom" or "brain break primary".

**Meta creative:** the 30-second clip from W3 (the teacher's classroom video). Targeting: parents 25–45 in the UK with kids aged 4–7, interest layer "primary education + parenting". Landing: `/parents/setup` with `?utm_source=meta&utm_medium=video&utm_campaign=w4_paid_test`. Spend cap: £15/day for 4 days.

**Google Search creative:** three text ads, three exact-match keywords. Keywords: `[movement breaks for class]`, `[brain break for primary]`, `[free classroom activities ks1]`. Landing for all three: `/teachers/setup` with utm tags. Cap: £10/day for 4 days.

**Measurement window:** 7 days from ad start. Success metrics in priority order: (1) one teacher who ran a real class from the campaign, (2) cost per teacher who reached `/teachers/setup` and clicked through to `/class`, (3) cost per `try_free_clicked` on the parents flow. If metric 1 is hit at any cost ≤£100, we keep spending. If only 2 and 3 are hit, we'd need 5x volume before we can attribute lift, so we wait and try again with the case study + organic stack at 3x size.

### Success criterion for W4

Parent share button is instrumented + emitting events. Case study CTA added below parent share. Both ad campaigns live with proper utm tagging, daily budget caps, and a 7-day measurement window scheduled.

## Beyond W4 — what comes next

If any of W1–W4 yields signal (a recurring teacher, a viral clip, a sub-£20 CAC, etc.), the next four weeks repeat that channel at 3x volume. If none yield signal — which would be surprising but possible — we re-examine the product-market fit assumptions, starting with: are we showing this to the right teacher level (Y1–Y3 vs Y4–Y6 vs nursery), is the right grown-up the buyer (teacher vs head vs IT lead), and is the activity menu the right cut (movement break vs phonics adjunct vs PE warm-up).

The point of running W1–W4 in one calendar month is to generate that signal fast — not to have everything working at scale immediately. The deliverable at the end of W4 is a clear answer to "which of these three channels (in-person, content, paid) is producing the first real classroom adoption?"

That answer is the input to the W5–W8 plan.

## Tracking

Single Sheet to keep: `Distribution_W1-W4.gsheet` with columns

| Date | Channel | Action | Output (URL / contact / response) | Result | Cost |

One row per outreach action, one row per content piece published, one row per ad created. Update at end of each day. Friday review: what got results, what didn't, what to repeat next week.

The goal at the end of W4 is not perfect attribution — it's directional learning. "In-person walk-ins produced 2 of 3 classroom adoptions; LinkedIn produced inbound but no closes; paid converted to traffic but not adoption" is a complete and actionable answer. Lean into whatever wins.
